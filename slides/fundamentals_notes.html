How can we quantify sustainability?


Press play at bottom of slide.

So given the agreed goal of sustainable development, we need to have some quantitative measure of sustainability.

Our values guide the manner we behave. Similarly, the methods by which we value resources (e.g., clean water and air, minerals, fossil fuels, solar insolation, etc.) profoundly influences the ways in which we choose to conserve or exploit them and even affects what we consider as a resource.

The economic or monetary value of a resource provides a useful measure by which everything can be compared on a "uniform", quantitative basis. It accounts for many factors, including the benefit of using the resource, the difficulty of its usage, its availability and demand, etc. It reflects our ability and desire to enact change. However, by its intrinsic nature, it is largely subjective and susceptible to manipulation (e.g., speculation in commodity markets, or changes in government policy). As a consequence, it does not provide an objective measure for assessing the environmental benefit or cost of exploiting a resource.

Another manner that is used to quantify sustainability is through the use of ecological footprints. These measures can be traced back to the idea of the carrying capacity of land. The use of concepts, such as carbon foot print or water footprint, provide a useful indication of sustainability, however, these typically only address a small number of specific issues, such as climate change, in limited circumstances and do not easily generalize to situations where multiple factors or objectives may be important (e.g., comparing the relative benefits of using nuclear power or the quantifying the relative advantages of recycling over waste to energy through incineration). There is arbitrariness in the manner in which one environmental issue is compared to another.

Thermodynamic approaches offer an objective and "universal" measure of the value of resources and the cost of various processes, through the concept of lost work or free energy. The value of a resource is directly related to its "work" (or exergy) content. The environmental impact of process is related to the amount thermodynamic work required to repair the damage done to the environment. Thermodynamic approaches have been used successfully to assess and improve the sustainability of industrial processes.

In this lecture, we will provide some of the background required for understanding and preforming thermodynamic analysis of systems. In particular, we will develop more fully the concept of thermodynamic work, and how it can be calculated. Finally, we will consider examples in how this can be used to compare the relative sustainability of different processes.

Hot cup of coffee: Extracting work

If we get in the way of the natural cooling of the cup of coffee, then we can extract work from it. As an example, we can use Stirling engine. By forcing the heat released from the coffee to pass through the engine before dissipating into the environment.

Not all the heat passing through an engine can be converted to work. This fraction is known as the efficiency of the engine. The maximum theoretical efficiency is given by the Carnot cycle, and it depends on the temperature at which the heat enters the engine and the temperature in which the engine releases the unused heat. The larger the temperature differential, the greater the theoretical efficiency.

Let's assume that our Stirling engine has the maximum theoretical efficiency. The Stirling engine receives heat from the hot cup of coffee, and it rejects the waste heat that it does not convert to work to the surrounding air. As the coffee cools, the temperature at which the Stirling engine receives heat will slowly drop, and as a result, its efficiency will decrease. Therefore, to determine the total amount of work that can be extracted over the entire cooling process, we need to integrate the efficiency with respect to the temperature of the coffee.

This leads to the final expression here. We recognize the first term in the expression for the work as the total enthalpy change of the coffee due to cooling. As mentioned previously, this is about 42 kJ. The second term is the portion of this heat that cannot be converted to work, due to the second law of thermodynamics.

For our 250 mL cup of coffee that started at 60^ˆ C, we find that we can potentially extract about 2.6 kJ of work, which is only about 6.3% of the heat released by the coffee. To put this into context, a AA alkaline battery contains about 14 kJ of energy. So we see that five or six of these cups of coffee would be about the same as a single AA battery.

In this analysis, we assumed that the Stirling engine ran with the maximum thermodynamic efficiency, so 2.6 kJ is the most we could ever expect. The formula that we derived is applicable to any substance with a constant heat capacity and can be used to estimate its work value. This is known as its thermal exergy and is only a property of the state of the material and its surroundings.

In reality, the true efficiency will be less than this, due practical issues, such as friction in the moving parts of the engine, loss of heat from the sides of the engine, and other factors. It has been quoted that the efficiency of low temperature differential Stirling engines is about a third of the Carnot efficiency. There are other engines that can be used to extract work from heat, such as thermoelectric generators, each with their own efficiencies and optimal range of conditions in which they work.

Exergy / availability

We have shown how to determine the maximum work that can be extracted from a system which is interacting with its surroundings, including materials flow streams.

What we want to do here is to use this expression to determine the maximum amount of work that we can extract from a system, which we will call its exergy. This can then be used to quantify the value of the system.

One manner to value a material is by the maximum amount of work that we can extract from it, which is known as its exergy or availability, and we denote this with the symbol \(\phi\). A material's exergy depends not only on its properties but also on the details of its surroundings.

So let's consider a system that is at pressure \(p\) and temperature \(T\), and is composed of a variety of species \(\alpha\) in amount \(N_\alpha\).

To calculate the maximum amount of work we can extract from the system, we consider a process where it exchanges heat, volume, and material with the surrounding environment until it has the same temperature, pressure and composition as the surrounding environment.

We divide this process into two distinct steps. In the first step, we exchange heat between the system and the environment, as well as expand or contract the system so that its temperature and pressure are equal to that of the environment. During this process, no material is exchanged between the system and the environment. So its composition will not be the same as the external environment.

In the second step, material is exchanged between the system and the environment until the composition of the system is identical to that of its surroundings. This may involve chemical reactions of components in the system.

This two step process divides the exergy into two contributions: physical exergy, which corresponds to the work extracted from the first step, and chemical exergy, which corresponds to the work extracted in the second step.

In this lecture, we examined some of the challenges in quantifying sustainability, and proposed using a thermodynamic approach.

We then examined the question of what we actually mean by energy conservation.

The first law states that energy is neither created nor destroyed, but is only converted to one form or another. However, the ease with which one form of energy can be converted to another is not the same. For example, it is easy to convert kinetic energy to heat but is more difficult to convert heat to kinetic energy. This is dictated by the second law of thermodynamics.

What is actually important to conserve is thermodynamic work, which is an idealized form of energy that can be perfectly and easily converted to any other form of energy. This is not conserved.

One of the key points to take away is that work can, in principle, be extracted from any process that occurs spontaneously (i.e., a process that will occur on its own). The maximum work that can be obtained from this process is the decrease in the free energy.

The reverse is also true. Non-spontaneous processes, that is processes that will not occur by themselves, can be made to happen by applying work. The minimum amount of work that is required is the increase in free energy.

Then we went through some examples to calculate thermodynamic work.

Finally, we presented a general method for calculating the maximum work that could be extracted from a system undergoing a change. In particular, we calculated the work content of a material, with respect to a specific environment.
