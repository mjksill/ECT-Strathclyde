So given the agreed goal of sustainable development, we need to have some quantitative measure of sustainability.

Our values guide the manner we behave. Similarly, the methods by which we value resources (e.g., clean water and air, minerals, fossil fuels, solar insolation, etc.) profoundly influences the ways in which we choose to conserve or exploit them and even affects what we consider as a resource.

The economic or monetary value of a resource provides a useful measure by which everything can be compared on a "uniform", quantitative basis. It accounts for many factors, including the benefit of using the resource, the difficulty of its usage, its availability and demand, etc. However, by its intrinsic nature, it is largely subjective and susceptible to manipulation (e.g., speculation in commodity markets, changes in government policy, etc.).

The use of concepts, such as carbon foot print or energy usage, provide a useful indication of sustainability, however, these typically only address a specific issues, such as climate change, in limited circumstances and do not easily generalize to situations where multiple factors may be important (e.g., comparing the relative benefits of using nuclear power or the quantifying the relative advantages of recycling over waste to energy through incineration).

Thermodynamic approaches offer an objective and "universal" measure of the value of resources and the cost of various processes, through the concept of lost work or free energy. The value of a resource is directly related to its "work" (exergy) content, and the cost of a process is the amount of lost work it incurs. Thermodynamic approaches have been used successfully to assess and improve the sustainability of industrial processes. However, a purely thermodynamic analysis has many shortcomings. For example, there is no consideration of the worth of time, such as the loss of value of resources that degrade or the time requirement for transformations to occur. In addition, they do not consider the availability and efficiency of technologies to perform particular transformations (e.g., if there is not technology available to extract it).

Hot cup of coffee

As a first example, let us consider the work content of a hot cup of coffee. Let's assume that the cup contains 250 mL of coffee and take its initial temperature to be 60^ˆ C and the temperature of the surrounding air to be 20^ˆ C. If left alone, we know that the coffee will eventually cool to 20 C, coming to thermal equilibrium with the surrounding air. Using the properties of water, we estimate that the coffee will release a total of 42 kJ.

The cooling of the coffee is a spontaneous process, which will occur on its own. According to our analysis, however, we know that this provides an opportunity to extract useful work.

Hot cup of coffee: Extracting work

If we get in the way of the natural cooling of the cup of coffee, then we can extract work from it. As an example, we can use Stirling engine. By forcing the heat released from the coffee to pass through the engine before dissipating into the environment.

Not all the heat passing through an engine can be converted to work. This fraction is known as the efficiency of the engine. The maximum theoretical efficiency is given by the Carnot cycle, and it depends on the temperature at which the heat enters the engine and the temperature in which the engine releases the unused heat. The larger the temperature differential, the greater the theoretical efficiency.

Let's assume that our Stirling engine has the maximum theoretical efficiency. The Stirling engine receives heat from the hot cup of coffee, and it rejects the waste heat that it does not convert to work to the surrounding air. As the coffee cools, the temperature at which the Stirling engine receives heat will slowly drop, and as a result, its efficiency will decrease. Therefore, to determine the total amount of work that can be extracted over the entire cooling process, we need to integrate the efficiency with respect to the temperature of the coffee.

This leads to the final expression here. We recognize the first term in the expression for the work as the total enthalpy change of the coffee due to cooling. As mentioned previously, this is about 42 kJ. The second term is the portion of this heat that cannot be converted to work, due to the second law of thermodynamics.

For our 250 mL cup of coffee that started at 60^ˆ C, we find that we can potentially extract about 2.6 kJ of work, which is only about 6.3% of the heat released by the coffee. To put this into context, a AA alkaline battery contains about 14 kJ of energy. So we see that five or six of these cups of coffee would be about the same as a single AA battery.

In this analysis, we assumed that the Stirling engine ran with the maximum thermodynamic efficiency, so 2.6 kJ is the most we could ever expect. The formula that we derived is applicable to any substance with a constant heat capacity and can be used to estimate its work value. This is known as its thermal exergy and is only a property of the state of the material and its surroundings.

In reality, the true efficiency will be less than this, due practical issues, such as friction in the moving parts of the engine, loss of heat from the sides of the engine, and other factors. It has been quoted that the efficiency of low temperature differential Stirling engines is about a third of the Carnot efficiency. There are other engines that can be used to extract work from heat, such as thermoelectric generators, each with their own efficiencies and optimal range of conditions in which they work.

Warm water or ice?

So now we have built a little intuition on the concept of exergy, let's consider a simple question that highlights the difference between exergy and energy. We want to deliver hot liquid water at 100^ˆ C, and we are given the choice of either starting with warm water at 60 C or ice at -20 C.

Which will require less fuel?

Warm water or ice: Exergy analysis

To get a clearer perspective of the problem, we plot the exergy of water as a function of temperature. The black curve is the exergy of liquid water. When the water is at ambient temperature, its exergy is zero, since it is at thermal equilibrium with its surroundings, and no work can be extracted from it. As the temperature of the water increases from the environmental temperature of 20^ˆ C, its exergy increases. This is expected, as it takes work to heat water.

The black dotted line represents the exergy content for water at 60 C, and the red dotted line represents the exergy content of liquid water at 100 C. So the minimum work required to heat liquid water from 60 C to 100 C is given by the distance between the red dotted line and the black dotted line, which is about 28.4 kJ/kg.

What might be somewhat unexpected is that the exergy of water increases as we cool it below ambient temperature. We can understand this if we take into account that it requires work to refrigerate things. So the work it takes to cool water below ambient temperature can be thought of as being stored in it as exergy.

The black curve continues to increase as the liquid water is cooled, until we reach 0 C, where it will start to freeze. The solid blue curve shows the variation of the exergy of ice with temperature. Note that there is a discontinuity between the black curve and the blue curve. This represents the work required to freeze water.

The exergy content of ice at -20 C is given by the blue dotted line. The work required to convert ice at this temperature to liquid water at 100 C is just the distance between the red dotted line and the blue dotted line, which is roughly 9.8 kJ/kg. This should be compared to the 28.4 kJ/kg of work required for water at 60 C. So in theory, we see that using ice will use about only a third of the fuel required by warm water.

While exergy analysis tells us that we can potentially save fuel by using ice, it does not tell us how we can practically do this. This requires ingenuity on our part. Working with the ice, we can use a Stirling engine, as we did with the hot cup of coffee, to extract work as it is melting. In this case, however, the engine will receive heat from the surrounding air and reject heat to the ice. In principle, we can run the engine until the ice fully melts and also while the resulting melt water warms up from 0 C to the ambient temperature. From there, we can then heat the water with a heat pump which is powered by the work we obtained fro the melting of the ice. If this process is run at full thermodynamic efficiency, it will take the temperature of the water up to over 80 C, where the blue dotted line intersects the black line in the exergy plot. Then, the remaining required heating can be done through the use of additional fuel.

Exergy / availability

  • Availability: the maximum amount of work that can be extracted from a closed system
  • Exergy: the maximum amount of work that can be extracted from a flowing system

We have shown how to determine the maximum work that can be extracted from a system which is interacting with its surroundings, including materials flow streams.

What we want to do here is to use this expression to determine the maximum amount of work that we can extract from a system, which we will call its exergy. This can then be used to quantify the value of the system.

One manner to value a material is by the maximum amount of work that we can extract from it, which is known as its exergy or availability, and we denote this with the symbol \(\phi\). A material's exergy depends not only on its properties but also on the details of its surroundings.

So let's consider a system that is at pressure \(p\) and temperature \(T\), and is composed of a variety of species \(\alpha\) in amount \(N_\alpha\).

To calculate the maximum amount of work we can extract from the system, we consider a process where it exchanges heat, volume, and material with the surrounding environment until it has the same temperature, pressure and composition as the surrounding environment.

We divide this process into two distinct steps. In the first step, we exchange heat between the system and the environment, as well as expand or contract the system so that its temperature and pressure are equal to that of the environment. During this process, no material is exchanged between the system and the environment. So its composition will not be the same as the external environment.

In the second step, material is exchanged between the system and the environment until the composition of the system is identical to that of its surroundings. This may involve chemical reactions of components in the system.

This two step process divides the exergy into two contributions: physical exergy, which corresponds to the work extracted from the first step, and chemical exergy, which corresponds to the work extracted in the second step.

Limitations of Exergy analysis

  • Only tells us what can be achieved, not how to achieve it.
  • Technology may not exist to perform required transformations
  • Neglects time
  • Assumes that the ``environment'' (reference state) is constant.
  • Neglects economic or social factors

Exergy is an extremely useful concept in assessing the sustainability of a process; however, as with all things, there are several limitations to exergy analysis. Even though

One of the key points to take away is that work can, in principle, be extracted from any process that occurs spontaneously (i.e., a process that will occur on its own). The maximum work that can be obtained from this process is the decrease in the free energy.

The reverse is also true. Non-spontaneous processes, that is processes that will not occur by themselves, can be made to happen by applying work. The minimum amount of work that is required is the increase in free energy.
